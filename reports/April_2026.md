## 1.2. Regulations, Standards & Guidance In the Works

### EU MDR/IVDR Simplification Proposal

**Reference:** Proposal 2025/0404 (COD) -- 16 December 2025
**Feedback period:** Until **6 May 2026**

Major proposed amendments to MDR (2017/745) and IVDR (2017/746):

- **Revised Rule 11 (software classification):** MDSW would by default be classified as **Class I** and increase in class according to the severity of the clinical situation, incorporating IMDRF SaMD WG/N12 terminology. This would remove notified body involvement for many software devices currently classified as IIa.
- **Removal of medical devices from AI Act high-risk scope:** AI-related requirements would be harmonized within MDR/IVDR rather than requiring dual compliance with the AI Act.
- Reduced re-certification obligations
- Expanded eIFU use
- Recognition of MDSAP participation
- Increased flexibility for in-house devices
- New pathways for orphan and breakthrough innovations

**Timeline:** Realistic adoption 2027 at the earliest.

## 1.4. Opinion -- Impact on MDSW Manufacturers

**EUDAMED (28 May 2026) -- Immediate operational priority.** All economic operators must obtain an SRN and complete Actor Registration before the deadline. For companies not yet registered, this is the most urgent action item. Existing devices must be registered by 28 November 2026.

**IEC 62304 Edition 2 -- Paradigm shift for software lifecycle management.** The move from three safety classes to two Process Accuracy Levels, combined with expanded scope to all health software, will require updates to software development procedures, risk classification documentation, and potentially QMS processes. Manufacturers should begin gap analysis now, even before formal publication. The inclusion of AI/ML process planning validates current industry practice but will formalize requirements that many companies have been handling informally.

**MDR/IVDR Simplification -- Game-changing for software classification, but don't celebrate yet.** The proposed Rule 11 revision could reclassify many MDSW from Class IIa to Class I, eliminating the need for notified body involvement. However, the legislative process is long (2027+ for adoption) and the final text may differ significantly from the proposal. Manufacturers should track this but not change their current classification strategy. The proposed removal of medical devices from the AI Act high-risk scope, if adopted, would dramatically simplify compliance for AI-based MDSW -- the current dual-compliance model (MDR + AI Act) is a significant burden.

**AI Act -- Prepare for complexity, hope for simplification.** The Digital Omnibus proposal may delay high-risk AI rules for medical devices to August 2028, and the MDR simplification proposal could remove MD from AI Act scope entirely. But neither is certain. Manufacturers should maintain their AI Act compliance roadmap (using MDCG 2025-6 as the primary reference) while monitoring the legislative trajectory. The failure of prEN 18286 at its first vote shows that harmonised standards for AI QMS are still maturing -- expect reliance on guidance documents and self-assessment in the interim.

**Cybersecurity is converging from multiple directions.** CRA (for non-MD software components), NIS2 (for manufacturers as entities), the ANSM cybersecurity recommendations, and IEC 81001-5-1 are all creating overlapping obligations. Manufacturers should build a unified cybersecurity management framework rather than addressing each regulation separately. ISO 27001:2022 provides a solid foundation with high overlap across these requirements.

**France: HDS v2.0 and sovereign cloud are non-negotiable for the French market.** The 16 May 2026 deadline for HDS v2.0 certification of hosting providers is imminent. The Health Data Hub migration to SecNumCloud signals that sovereign hosting will become the de facto standard for health data in France. SaaS-based MDSW manufacturers must verify their hosting infrastructure now.

## 2.2. In the Works & Announcements

### MHRA Consultation: Indefinite CE Mark Recognition

**Consultation opened:** 16 February 2026 | **Closed:** 10 April 2026

The MHRA proposes **indefinite recognition of CE-marked medical devices** compliant with EU MDR/IVDR in Great Britain, without additional regulatory steps. For devices still under old EU MDD, the transitional period would be extended to 31 December 2028. ~90% of devices currently on the GB market are CE-marked.

**Impact:** If adopted, this would eliminate the need for separate UKCA conformity assessment for EU MDR/IVDR-compliant devices, dramatically simplifying dual market access. SaMD/AIaMD may face additional scrutiny under Route 4 of the international reliance framework.

### Pre-Market Statutory Instrument (Second Major SI)

**Status:** Drafting/pre-parliamentary stage; WTO notification expected 2026; implementation likely late 2026 or 2027.

Key elements:
- **Up-classification of SaMD** to at least Class IIa (mirroring EU MDR approach), requiring UK Approved Body conformity assessment
- International reliance scheme (SaMD/AIaMD eligible only for Route 4, the most scrutinised route)
- Unique Device Identification (UDI)
- Expanded technical documentation requirements
- New IVD classification rules

### National Commission on AI in Healthcare

**Status:** Call for evidence closed 2 February 2026; recommendations expected later in 2026.

Chaired by Prof. Alastair Denniston. Developing a regulatory rulebook for AI in healthcare covering: framework improvements for safe/effective AI medical devices, post-market safety monitoring, responsibility/liability allocation. Will shape the UK's bespoke AI medical device framework.

### ICO Focus on AI/Automated Decision-Making

The ICO has identified automated decision-making (ADM) and AI governance as a priority enforcement area for 2026. AI-based SaMD making or supporting clinical decisions must ensure UK GDPR compliance around ADM, transparency, and DPIAs.

## 2.3. Opinion -- Impact on MDSW Manufacturers

**The indefinite CE mark recognition proposal is the single most important UK development for EU-based MDSW manufacturers.** If adopted, it would remove the dual certification burden that has been a major pain point since Brexit. However, the outcome is not guaranteed, and SaMD may be subject to more stringent requirements under Route 4 of the reliance scheme. Manufacturers should continue preparing for UKCA compliance until the final decision is published.

**SaMD up-classification is coming.** The second Statutory Instrument will align UK classification with EU MDR for software. Companies currently marketing Class I SaMD in GB should prepare for Class IIa or higher classification, including identifying a UK Approved Body. The limited number of UK Approved Bodies (far fewer than EU Notified Bodies) may create capacity bottlenecks.

**The UK-US regulatory cooperation is strategic but not yet operational.** While mutual recognition between MHRA and FDA would be transformative, concrete mechanisms are still being explored. This is a medium-term development to monitor.

**The Data (Use and Access) Act creates new obligations for NHS-facing software.** Interoperability requirements and information standards will affect MDSW that exchanges data within the NHS ecosystem. The broadened research definition may facilitate real-world evidence generation for clinical evaluation.

## 3.3. Opinion -- Impact on MDSW Manufacturers

**QMSR is a major harmonization win for EU-based manufacturers.** Companies already ISO 13485:2016 certified have a structural advantage for US market access. However, FDA-specific requirements in the QMSR still need attention -- ISO 13485 compliance alone is not sufficient. The new risk-based inspection approach (replacing QSIT) changes how FDA inspections are conducted.

**CSA replaces CSV -- a welcome simplification.** The risk-based Computer Software Assurance approach significantly reduces documentation burden for low-risk production/QMS software while maintaining rigor for high-risk features. Companies should update their computer system validation SOPs to adopt CSA principles. Note that this applies to production and QMS software, not device software functions.

**The deregulatory posture under Commissioner Makary creates opportunity and uncertainty.** The expanded CDS and general wellness exemptions, combined with the withdrawal of the IMDRF-based SaMD clinical evaluation guidance, signal a deliberate reduction of FDA oversight over lower-risk digital health technologies. This creates market opportunities for EU manufacturers entering the US. However, the withdrawal of the IMDRF guidance without replacement creates a gap in clinical evaluation methodology -- manufacturers should document their clinical evaluation approach carefully.

**HIPAA modernization will impact SaaS architectures.** The proposed Security Rule overhaul makes MFA, encryption, and 24-hour breach reporting mandatory. Digital health companies with cloud-based architectures should begin preparing now, as the May 2026 finalization target approaches.

**PCCP for AI/ML is the most forward-looking FDA initiative.** The finalized AI-specific PCCP guidance allows manufacturers to pre-define algorithm modifications and validation methodology, avoiding new submissions for each planned change. This is a competitive advantage for AI-based MDSW manufacturers who invest in robust change management frameworks.

## 4.2. Opinion -- Impact on MDSW Manufacturers

**South Korea is the most dynamic jurisdiction.** Three major regulatory developments in January 2026 alone. The fast-track pathway for digital devices (80-140 days vs. 490) and the DMPA pre-approved change management framework make South Korea increasingly attractive for AI-based MDSW market entry. Manufacturers targeting Asia-Pacific should prioritize South Korea.

**China's YY/T 1406-2026 signals a paradigm shift in risk management.** The integration of functional safety and cybersecurity into a unified framework, with system-level requirements (cloud, mobile, interoperability), aligns with the international direction (IEC 81001-5-1). Manufacturers already following EU cybersecurity requirements will have a head start.

**UDI is going global.** Australia (July 2026), Brazil (phased 2026-2028), and Saudi Arabia (April 2026) are all implementing UDI. Manufacturers should establish a global UDI strategy rather than addressing each jurisdiction separately.

**Singapore's AI-SaMD sandbox is a regulatory innovation model.** The approach of allowing real-world deployment under controlled conditions before full registration could influence other jurisdictions. The AIHGle 2.0 guidelines, with their seven ethical principles including specific generative AI provisions, represent one of the most comprehensive AI healthcare governance frameworks globally.

**FDA withdrawal of IMDRF SaMD clinical evaluation guidance creates a divergence.** While the IMDRF framework remains the international reference (used by TGA, Health Canada, and to some extent by EU NBs), the US now diverges. Manufacturers targeting multiple markets should maintain their IMDRF-aligned clinical evaluation approach for non-US markets while adapting to FDA's evolving expectations for the US.
